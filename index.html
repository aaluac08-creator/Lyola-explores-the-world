Iran's Regional Tapestry

Each region has its own landscape, food, and people — making the country feel more like a collection of small worlds stitched together.

Region 1

Northwest

Azerbaijani Influenced Region

Cities: Tabriz, Ardabil, Zanjan, Urmia

Mountainous and green compared to the deserts, this is the homeland of Azerbaijanis, who speak Azerbaijani Turkish and make up about 16–20% of the population. The city of Tabriz is their cultural capital.

This region has strong ties to the Caucasus and Turkey, and Azeri traditions (like music, cuisine, and even language) are woven into Iran's identity.

Region 2

West

Kurdistan & Kermanshah

Population: ~10% of Iran

Rugged Zagros Mountains dominate here, home to the Kurds, who make up around 10% of the population. Villages cling to the slopes, and Kurdish culture is known for lively dances, colorful clothes, and a long history of independence movements.

Region 3

Southwest

Khuzestan Province & Persian Gulf Coast

Population: ~2% Iranian Arabs

Hot, humid, and oil-rich. Iranian Arabs live here, especially near the Iraq border and the Gulf. The culture mixes Arab and Persian influences — you'll find Arabic spoken alongside Persian, seafood-heavy cuisine, and Gulf-style traditions.

Region 4

Central Iran

The Persian Cultural Heartland

Cities: Tehran, Isfahan, Yazd, Shiraz, Kashan

This is the Persian cultural heartland. Persians dominate here, and cities like Isfahan ("Half the World") showcase stunning Islamic architecture.

Shiraz is famous for poetry, gardens, and wine (before the ban). Yazd is a desert city with windcatchers and ancient Zoroastrian fire temples. And Tehran, the capital, is a chaotic mix of tradition and modernity.

Region 5

West-Central

Lorestan, Ilam, Chaharmahal, Kohgiluyeh

Population: ~6% Lurs

Mountainous and tribal, this is the land of the Lurs, a people related to Persians but with their own dialect and culture. Known for hospitality, storytelling, and traditional music, many still keep semi-nomadic traditions alive.

Region 6

Southeast

Sistan & Baluchistan

Population: ~2% Baluchis

One of Iran's driest, least developed regions, home to the Baluchis. Their culture, clothing, and language connect them more with Pakistan and Afghanistan. Despite hardship, Baluch hospitality and traditions stand strong.

Region 7

Northeast

Khorasan – Mashhad & Turkmen Sahra

Two worlds in one: Mashhad, Iran's second city, is the center of Shia pilgrimage thanks to Imam Reza's shrine.

Further north in Golestan, the Turkmens live in steppe lands, famous for horse breeding, carpets, and Central Asian traditions.

Region 8

Northern Iran

Caspian Region – Gilan & Mazandaran

Lush, rainy, and green — this region feels like another planet compared to Iran's deserts. Rice paddies, forests, and caviar come from here.

The people, called Gilaks and Mazandaranis, have their own languages and traditions. Iranians from other regions often come here for summer holidays.

Region 9

The Deserts

Dasht-e Lut & Dasht-e Kavir

The extreme side of Iran: salt flats, shifting dunes, and some of the hottest recorded temperatures on Earth.

Few people live here, but the landscapes look straight out of a sci-fi movie.

Part 3

The People of Iran

Iran's ethnic diversity is as rich as its landscapes. From Persians to Azeris, Kurds to Baluchis, each group contributes to the nation's cultural tapestry.

Ethnic Breakdown

Population by ethnic group

A Diverse Nation

The ethnic breakdown all around Iran reveals a fascinating diversity that many outsiders don't expect.

Persians: 61%

Persians are around 61% of the population, forming the largest ethnic group. They primarily speak Farsi (Persian) and are concentrated in central and southern Iran.

Azeris: 16%

Azeris are about 16%, mainly in the northwest. Fun fact: Iran's Supreme Leader, Ali Khamenei, is Azeri.

Kurds: 10%

Kurds are around 10%, mostly in western Iran. Known for their vibrant culture, colorful traditional clothing, and distinctive music.

Lurs: 6%

Lurs are roughly 6%. Related to Persians but with their own dialect and rich traditions of hospitality and storytelling.

Arabs: 2%

Arabs are about 2%, near the Persian Gulf. Their culture blends Arab and Persian influences, with Arabic spoken alongside Farsi.

Baluchis: 2%

Baluchis are about 2%, in the southeast. Their culture connects more with Pakistan and Afghanistan than central Iran.

Turkmen & Others: 3%

Turkmen and others are around 2–3%. This includes various smaller ethnic groups throughout the country.

Linguistic Diversity

While Persian (Farsi) is official, many people speak Azerbaijani, Kurdish, Arabic, Baluchi, and more.

Iran is truly a multilingual nation, with regional languages thriving alongside the official language.

Part 4

The Islamic Regime

How did a country with so many secular-minded people become known for its strict Islamic laws? Let's explore the 1979 Revolution and Iran's unique political system.

Religious Landscape

85-90% Shia Muslim

Iran is the world's largest Shia-majority country

10-15% Sunni & Others

Including Kurds, Baluchis, and minority religions

Important context: Many Iranians, especially young people and those living abroad, are secular. Even inside Iran, people often follow Islam more as culture than as strict religion.

The 1979 Islamic Revolution

A pivotal moment that transformed Iran from monarchy to Islamic Republic

Before 1979

The Monarchy

Iran was ruled by Shah Mohammad Reza Pahlavi. The period saw modernization but also political repression, corruption, and Western influence that alienated many Iranians.

1979

The Revolution

Mass protests and strikes led by Ayatollah Khomeini overthrew the monarchy. The revolution united diverse groups seeking change, from religious conservatives to leftists and liberals.

After 1979

Islamic Republic

Iran transformed into an Islamic Republic, combining religious leadership with democratic elements. The system consolidated power under religious authority.

Part 5

Iran's Goofy & Unexpected Side

Beyond politics and poetry, Iran has a delightfully quirky everyday culture.

Nose Job Capital of the World

Iran performs more rhinoplasties per capita than any country. Bandages are worn like badges of honor—even faked as fashion!

~150,000+ surgeries/year

Ice Cream... With Noodles?

Faloodeh: frozen rice noodles soaked in rosewater, lime, and sugar. Served with saffron ice cream—golden, fragrant, and iconic.

Tarof – The Politeness Game

A social ritual where you refuse what you want—twice—before accepting. "No, no, I insist!" is the national sport of hospitality.

Last Wild Asiatic Cheetahs

Fewer than 12 remain—only in Iran. A national symbol of fragility and pride.

Critically Endangered

The Peykan – National Car

A 1970s boxy sedan, still running on nostalgia and spare parts. Cultural icon of resilience.

Selfies with Road Signs

Iranian tourists abroad love posing with signs like "Berlin" or "Paris"—a quirky travel ritual turned meme.
